# ENERGY STAR for Commercial Buildings

## Overview
ENERGY STAR is the EPA's voluntary program for energy efficiency. The ENERGY STAR score (1–100) benchmarks a building's energy performance against similar buildings nationwide. A score of 50 is median; a score of 75 or above earns ENERGY STAR certification.

## Scoring Methodology
- Based on **source EUI** (Energy Use Intensity) — total energy per square foot per year
- Normalized for: weather (climate zone), operating hours, occupancy, building size
- Uses data from the Commercial Buildings Energy Consumption Survey (CBECS)
- Entered and tracked through **ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager**

## Business Value
- **Rental premium**: ENERGY STAR buildings command 3–5% higher rents
- **Occupancy rates**: 2–4% higher occupancy
- **Operating costs**: 20–35% lower energy costs than non-certified buildings
- **Transaction value**: Properties with ENERGY STAR certification sell at 10–15% premium
- **Tenant demand**: Major corporate tenants increasingly require ENERGY STAR certified spaces

## Improvement Strategies (by score band)
### Score 1–25 (Poor performer)
- Immediate: Retro-commissioning, plug load management, schedule optimization
- Short-term: LED lighting retrofit, HVAC tune-up
- Medium-term: Equipment replacement, envelope improvements
- Target: 20–40 point improvement in 2–3 years

### Score 25–50 (Below average)
- BMS optimization and fault detection
- HVAC system upgrades (VFDs, economizers)
- Lighting controls (occupancy, daylight harvesting)
- Target: 15–25 point improvement in 1–2 years

### Score 50–74 (Average to good)
- Fine-tune BMS setpoints and schedules
- Advanced controls (demand response, load shifting)
- Renewable energy procurement
- Target: Reach 75+ for ENERGY STAR certification

### Score 75+ (Excellent)
- Maintain certification through continuous commissioning
- Pursue LEED certification for additional market differentiation
- Consider net-zero pathway
